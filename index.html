Zero point ¬∑ Before everything

Decide: Synthesis-Based Review or Narrative Opinion Piece Non-negotiable

The PDF is clear: many ‚Äúreviews‚Äù in journals are actually loose narrative summaries with selective evidence and weak appraisal. They are easy to write and easy to criticize. If your PhD review is narrative-style, it is not strong evidence. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Avoid Narrative / ‚Äújust a literature review‚Äù
  • Studies picked informally (‚ÄúI chose the main papers I know‚Äù).
  • No explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria.
  • Search often limited to one database or even just Google Scholar.
  • Little or no formal quality appraisal.
  • High risk of cherry-picking and personal bias.
Example ‚Äì weak narrative PhD review

‚ÄúI searched Google Scholar with two keywords and selected 30 articles that seemed important. Then I grouped them and described the findings.‚Äù This sounds simple, but it will not pass as serious evidence synthesis in a tough viva.

Use Synthesis-based review (what this blueprint supports)
  • Pre-planned protocol and review question.
  • Transparent multi-database search, documented and reproducible.
  • Clear inclusion/exclusion rules, applied systematically.
  • Formal appraisal of study quality / risk of bias.
  • Structured synthesis (meta-analysis or organized narrative).
  • Reporting guided by standards like PRISMA, IOM, MECIR.
Example ‚Äì synthesis-based PhD review

You write a protocol, run a documented search in four databases with librarian input, use two reviewers for screening, extract data into standardized tables, appraise each study, and synthesize results using methods planned in advance. Anyone can replay your steps.

Hard line: If you want your review to be strong evidence, you must treat it as a synthesis project, not as a ‚Äúreading summary‚Äù. That is the entire logic of the PDF you gave. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
Phase 0 ¬∑ Typology

Review Typology Dashboard: Pick the Right Method From the PDF table

The PDF lists common synthesis methods (meta-analysis, systematic, scoping, rapid, mapping, umbrella) and explains how they differ in search depth, appraisal, and synthesis style. This dashboard turns that into a UI you can act on directly.

MA Meta-analysis
  • Goal: Combine effect sizes statistically to give a pooled estimate.
  • Search: Full systematic search across multiple databases, unbiased.
  • Protocol: Normally required and written in advance.
  • Appraisal: Formal quality and risk-of-bias assessment for each study.
  • Synthesis: Statistical pooling, forest plots, effect sizes, confidence intervals.
  • Visuals: Forest plots, tables, graphs to show combined impact.
When to use

You have several trials that measure the same outcome (for example HbA1c, depression score, falls). You want ‚Äúwhat is the best estimate across all reliable studies?‚Äù.

SR Systematic review
  • Goal: Summarize and appraise all relevant evidence using structured methods.
  • Search: Comprehensive, unbiased, documented search.
  • Protocol: Planned in advance, ideally registered.
  • Appraisal: Explicit quality assessment.
  • Synthesis: Tabular + narrative; may or may not include meta-analysis.
  • Visuals: Summary tables, graphs, PRISMA flow chart.
When to use

You want to answer a focused question (‚ÄúDoes X improve Y for Z?‚Äù) but outcomes or methods differ slightly between studies, or data are not easily pooled.

SCOPING Scoping review
  • Goal: Map key concepts, evidence types, and gaps.
  • Search: Broad, still systematic and as unbiased as possible.
  • Protocol: Often flexible; not always mandatory, but still wise.
  • Appraisal: Not always required, but strongly recommended.
  • Synthesis: Mainly descriptive tables and narrative mapping.
  • Visuals: Concept maps, tables showing where evidence exists and where it doesn‚Äôt.
When to use

Your topic is broad and emerging, for example ‚ÄúHow are AI tools used to support PhD students?‚Äù You want to see what is out there before doing a very focused systematic review.

RAPID Rapid review
  • Goal: Give a timely picture of what is known on a policy/practice issue.
  • Search: Streamlined but still systematic and transparent.
  • Protocol: Brief protocol describing shortcuts used.
  • Appraisal: Often simplified but not ignored.
  • Synthesis: Condensed narrative + key tables.
When to use

Decision-makers need an answer in months, not years ‚Äì for example ‚ÄúWhat do we know about online learning during lockdowns?‚Äù.

MAPPING Mapping review
  • Goal: Categorize existing studies and highlight research gaps.
  • Search: Shaped by time and scope limits but still systematic.
  • Appraisal: Usually no detailed quality rating.
  • Synthesis: Tables and figures showing study types, topics, populations.
When to use

You want a ‚Äúmap of the battlefield‚Äù, for example which populations and outcomes are covered in fall-prevention research, and which are ignored.

UMBRELLA Umbrella / Overview review
  • Goal: Summarize what multiple systematic reviews/meta-analyses together show.
  • Search: Focused on finding review articles, not individual trials.
  • Protocol: Planned a priori, includes how reviews will be appraised (AMSTAR, ROBIS). :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
  • Appraisal: Quality of the included reviews themselves is rated.
  • Synthesis: Graphical and tabular summary across reviews.
When to use

The field is already full of systematic reviews. You want a ‚Äúreview of reviews‚Äù ‚Äì for example all existing systematic reviews on exercise and falls in older adults.

üß≠ Typology decision ‚Äì you are not allowed to stay vague
‚úî You can name exactly which type you are doing (e.g., ‚Äúsystematic review‚Äù, ‚Äúscoping review‚Äù).
‚úî Your chosen type matches your question and the maturity of the field.
‚úî Your methods later in the paper match the expectations for that type (search, appraisal, synthesis).
‚úî You can defend, in one sentence, why you did this type and not another.

Five-Phase Review Engine (Directly Reflecting the PDF Figure) Process spine

The PDF breaks a good review into five phases: Protocol, Search, Inclusion, Data, Report. This is your backbone. Every serious review method you pick must pass through these stages.

Pipeline Phase summary

  2. Search: Define concepts and databases, build strategy with librarian, manage citations.
  3. Inclusion: Apply criteria, record reasons for exclusion, keep a clear database trail.
  4. Data: Extract data using a predefined form and appraise quality / risk of bias.
  5. Report: Synthesize, interpret strengths/limitations, make recommendations, publish.

See deep explanation + example for each phase

Phase 1 ‚Äì Protocol

  • Set up your team (you, supervisor, second reviewer, librarian if possible).
  • Write the review question using PICOS (or PCC for scoping).
  • Write a protocol that covers eligibility, search, screening, extraction, appraisal, synthesis.
  • Register or publicly time-stamp the protocol (PROSPERO, OSF, or journal protocol article).
Example ‚Äì PhD mental health review

You decide to do a scoping review on digital tools for PhD student mental health. Your protocol states exactly who counts as ‚ÄúPhD student‚Äù, what counts as ‚Äúdigital tool‚Äù, which outcomes you accept (depression, anxiety, stress scores), which databases you will search, and that you will chart data into predefined tables without doing a meta-analysis.

Phase 2 ‚Äì Search

  • Break your question into core concepts.
  • Work with a librarian to expand concepts into controlled terms + keywords.
  • Run searches in all databases you promised, record search dates and strings.
  • Export results, remove duplicates, keep a search log.
Example ‚Äì concepts for PhD mental health

Concepts: (1) doctoral students, (2) mental health, (3) digital tools. For each you build synonyms (e.g., ‚Äúdoctoral candidate‚Äù, ‚ÄúPhD student‚Äù, etc.) and combine with AND/OR across databases. You save every final string in an appendix.

Phase 3 ‚Äì Inclusion

  • Two reviewers independently screen titles/abstracts.
  • Two reviewers screen full texts and decide include/exclude.
  • For every excluded full text, you log a precise reason (wrong population, wrong design, etc.).
  • Later, this feeds the PRISMA flow diagram.
Example ‚Äì tough but fair decision

A paper looks ‚Äúperfect‚Äù conceptually but actually includes mixed undergraduates and PhD students, without separate data. Your protocol says you only include studies where you can extract data for PhD students. You therefore exclude it, and you log the reason as ‚Äúmixed population, PhD data not separable‚Äù. That‚Äôs discipline.

Phase 4 ‚Äì Data

  • You extract all predefined variables into structured tables.
  • You run your chosen risk-of-bias or quality assessment tool on each study.
  • You do not change your extraction items in the middle unless you update the protocol.
Example ‚Äì extraction form

For each study on PhD writing bootcamps you capture: study design, number of participants, country, bootcamp format, length, outcomes (words written, chapters finished, self-reported confidence), follow-up length, risk-of-bias rating. All in one table.

Phase 5 ‚Äì Report

  • Decide if a meta-analysis is justified; if not, structure a narrative synthesis.
  • Write methods fully so someone else can repeat your work.
  • Summarize results with tables, graphs, and clear text.
  • Point out strengths and limitations honestly, including any deviations from protocol.
Example ‚Äì honest limitation

You planned to include all languages, but in reality you only screened English and French. You admit this clearly in the methods and discussion, rather than pretending the review is language-free.

Connect to standards

PRISMA and IOM standards literally mirror this five-phase logic: protocol, search, selection, synthesis, reporting. Using them is not ‚Äúextra work‚Äù, it is just doing these phases properly and documenting them.

Phase 1 ¬∑ People

Minimum Review Team and Role Layout Who does what

The PDF directly recommends involving a librarian and a review-methods expert where possible. A lonely PhD doing everything in isolation is a red flag; build at least a ‚Äúlightweight‚Äù team around you. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Core roles Minimal viable team
  • You (PhD): topic expert, driver of all work.
  • Supervisor: content + methods oversight; signs off on protocol, keeps you honest.
  • Second reviewer: independent screening and risk-of-bias checks.
  • Librarian / information specialist: designs and refines your search; helps build search strategy appendix.
  • Statistician (if doing MA): supports effect size selection, models, heterogeneity tests.
Example ‚Äì practical setup

You + supervisor handle the main work. A more senior PhD or postdoc serves as second reviewer. The university library offers a 1-hour consultation; you use that to clean up the search and then ask the librarian to check your strings before you lock them in the protocol.

Reality If you truly have to work alone
  • Do a ‚Äúdouble screen‚Äù of a random subset at two different times to check your own consistency.
  • Ask your supervisor at least to review inclusion/exclusion rules and one sample of decisions.
  • Be explicit in the paper that screening and appraisal were done by one reviewer due to constraints.
‚ö†Ô∏è
Important: hiding that you were the only reviewer is worse than admitting it. Reviewers are more forgiving when you are honest about constraints.
Phase 2 ¬∑ Aim

Build a Laser-Focused Review Question (PICOS / PCC) Target lock

A fuzzy question produces a fuzzy review. The PDF stresses that synthesis-based reviews must answer focused, clearly formulated questions using explicit methods. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

For effect / intervention questions (PICOS)

  • P ‚Äì Population
  • I ‚Äì Intervention / exposure
  • C ‚Äì Comparison
  • O ‚Äì Outcome(s)
  • S ‚Äì Study design(s) allowed
Example Falls and exercise
  • P: community-dwelling adults aged ‚â•65.
  • I: supervised group exercise ‚â•8 weeks.
  • C: usual care or no organized exercise.
  • O: number of falls, balance scores.
  • S: randomized controlled trials.
Final question

‚ÄúAmong community-dwelling adults aged 65 and older, do supervised group exercise programs, compared with usual care or no structured exercise, reduce falls and improve balance?‚Äù

Example PhD productivity (directly useful to your audience)
  • P: PhD students in any discipline.
  • I: structured writing groups or bootcamps.
  • C: usual unstructured writing (no organized support).
  • O: completed chapters, words written, papers submitted, writing confidence.
  • S: experimental, quasi-experimental, longitudinal cohort.
Final question

‚ÄúWhat is the effect of structured writing groups and bootcamps, compared to usual unstructured writing practice, on thesis progress, publication output, and writing confidence among PhD students?‚Äù

For broader scoping questions (PCC style)

  • P ‚Äì Population (who)
  • C ‚Äì Concept (key idea you are mapping)
  • C ‚Äì Context (setting / field)
Example ‚Äì scoping review, digital mental health for PhDs

Population: PhD / doctoral students. Concept: digital tools for mental health support. Context: university and doctoral training environments. Question: ‚ÄúHow have digital tools been used to support mental health in PhD students, and what outcomes have been measured?‚Äù

Phase 3 ¬∑ Commit

Write and Register a Protocol + Connect to Reporting Standards Guard rails

The PDF makes it clear: high-quality reviews are planned, not improvised. Protocol + standards (PRISMA, IOM, Cochrane MECIR, EQUATOR, ICMJE) are your guard rails.

Protocol contents What you write before touching data
  • Title with review type in it (e.g., ‚ÄúA systematic review‚Äù, ‚ÄúA scoping review‚Äù).
  • Background + reason this review is needed now.
  • Exact review question(s) using PICOS/PCC.
  • Eligibility criteria (population, intervention, outcomes, designs, languages, dates, settings).
  • Planned information sources and databases.
  • Initial search strategy for at least one database.
  • Screening process (number of reviewers, how disagreements are handled).
  • Data items you will extract (variables list).
  • Quality / risk-of-bias tools you will use.
  • Synthesis methods (meta-analysis rules, subgroup plans, or narrative templates).
Standards dock Key frameworks mentioned in the PDF
  • PRISMA ‚Äì reporting checklist + flow diagram for systematic reviews and meta-analyses.
  • Cochrane MECIR ‚Äì expectations for how to conduct and report intervention reviews.
  • IOM standards ‚Äì full standards for systematic reviews (search, selection, synthesis, reporting).
  • EQUATOR network ‚Äì hub for reporting guidelines (CONSORT, STROBE, PRISMA, etc.).
  • ICMJE + COPE ‚Äì ethics and manuscript guidelines also valid for review papers.
In simple terms: your review lives inside this ecosystem. You don‚Äôt have to memorize everything, but you should at least show that your protocol respects these principles.

Phase 4 & 5 ¬∑ Evidence in, noise out

Search Strategy UI + Screening & PRISMA Logic Evidence funnel

This is where most students quietly cut corners. The PDF is explicit: searches must be comprehensive, unbiased, and documented; selection must list reasons for exclusion and end up in PRISMA-style reporting.

Search builder Databases + query design
  • Pick at least 2‚Äì4 core databases that fit your field.
  • Split your question into concepts and write keywords + subject headings for each.
  • Test the search with a small set of ‚Äúmust-find‚Äù known key papers.
  • Record final strings and dates for each database for the appendix.
Example ‚Äì concept expansion for PhD mental health

Concept 1 (population): ‚Äúdoctoral student*‚Äù, ‚ÄúPhD student*‚Äù, ‚Äúpostgraduate research student*‚Äù. Concept 2 (mental health): ‚Äúdepression‚Äù, ‚Äúanxiety‚Äù, ‚Äústress‚Äù, ‚Äúpsychological distress‚Äù. Concept 3 (delivery): ‚Äúapp‚Äù, ‚Äúonline program*‚Äù, ‚Äúweb-based‚Äù, ‚Äúmobile‚Äù.   3. Full-text screening on all ‚Äúinclude‚Äù and ‚Äúunsure‚Äù.

Risk-of-bias layer (why it matters)

Risk-of-bias tools help you label how trustworthy each study is. High risk does not mean you must ignore a study, but you should weigh it carefully in your interpretation.

Tools Match to design
  • Randomized trials ‚Äì tools like RoB 2.
  • Non-randomized interventions ‚Äì tools like ROBINS-I.
  • Observational studies ‚Äì checklists (for example, Newcastle-Ottawa-type logic).
  • Qualitative studies ‚Äì frameworks like CASP for qualitative research.
You don‚Äôt have to use these exact labels in your PhD if your field uses other tools, but you must show that you are judging quality in a structured way.

Phase 8 ¬∑ Putting it together

Synthesis UI: Meta-analysis vs Structured Narrative From numbers to meaning

If meta-analysis is possible Statistical synthesis
  • Check whether outcomes and measures are similar enough to be pooled.
  • Calculate effect sizes per study (risk ratios, mean differences, standardized differences, etc.).
  • Select a model (often random-effects in practice).
  • Measure heterogeneity (I¬≤) and interpret it.
  • Produce forest plots and report pooled effects with confidence intervals.
Example ‚Äì older adult exercise

‚ÄúTen randomized trials with 1,200 participants show that supervised group exercise reduces falls by roughly 23% (pooled risk ratio 0.77, 95% CI 0.65‚Äì0.91); statistical heterogeneity was modest (I¬≤ around 30%).‚Äù

If meta-analysis is not realistic Narrative synthesis
  • Group studies into logical clusters (by intervention type, population, outcome, or context).
  • Describe the direction and approximate size of effect, not just ‚Äúsignificant or not‚Äù.
  • Bring quality judgements into the story (‚Äúmost positive findings were from weaker studies‚Äù).
  • Use summary tables and figures to support the text.
Example ‚Äì writing support for PhD students

‚ÄúThree randomized trials and four weaker quasi-experimental studies evaluated structured writing groups. Trials showed small-to-moderate improvements in short-term writing output, but none examined outcomes beyond three months. The weaker studies reported positive experiences but had serious design limitations. Overall, there is reasonable evidence for short-term productivity gains, but evidence about long-term impact and thesis completion is thin.‚Äù

When you write your discussion and conclusion, you must explicitly connect your claims to where your included evidence sits on this pyramid and how strong your synthesis actually is.

Example ‚Äì linking conclusion to evidence strength

‚ÄúBecause most included PhD mental health studies were small pre-post designs without control groups, the overall certainty is low. Even though short-term changes look positive, we cannot be confident that the interventions cause the improvements. Stronger controlled trials are clearly needed.‚Äù

Phase 10 ¬∑ Final output

Writing the Review: Structure + ‚ÄúNo-Surprises‚Äù Checklist Delivery

Structure that journals and examiners expect

  1. Title ‚Äì states topic and review type clearly.
  2. Abstract ‚Äì structured; includes key methods and main findings.
  3. Introduction ‚Äì sets up problem, gap, and your review question.
  4. Methods ‚Äì protocol, eligibility, search, selection, extraction, appraisal, synthesis.
  5. Results ‚Äì PRISMA flow, characteristics tables, risk-of-bias summary, outcome-wise findings.
  6. Discussion ‚Äì main messages, comparison with earlier work, limitations, implications.
  7. Conclusion ‚Äì specific, evidence-proportionate final statements.
  8. Extras ‚Äì full search strings, detailed tables, checklists as appendices.
Strong vs weak conclusion side-by-side
Weak conclusion

‚ÄúDigital tools seem promising for PhD mental health, but more research is needed.‚Äù

Vague, generic, could be written for any topic. No sense of effect size, certainty, or who this applies to.

Strong conclusion

‚ÄúAcross 11 small, mostly uncontrolled studies with 430 PhD students, digital mental health tools were associated with short-term reductions in self-reported stress and anxiety. Because almost all studies were at serious risk of bias and none followed participants beyond three months, current evidence is low-certainty. Institutions should treat these tools as promising but unproven and prioritize better-designed trials before large-scale roll-out.‚Äù
