Why this matters

Clear writing shows your own mind. Bad copying breaks trust fast.

What happens if you ignore this:
  • Your paper can get rejected.
  • Your name can be blocked by journals.
  • Your work loses value because no one trusts it.
  • Your career takes real damage because retractions stay online forever.
Easy truth: If you give credit and use your own words, you are safe. If you hide ideas, problems grow.

Kinds of plagiarism

Use this list to classify every match you see.

  • Word copy: same words, no quotes.
  • Patch copy: swap a few words but keep same shape.
  • Self-copy: use your old lines with no note.
  • Idea steal: take someone’s core idea.
  • Data steal: take numbers, tables, charts.
  • Image reuse: use figures without permission.
Note: Some copying is small in words but huge in meaning. Always check the idea, not just the phrasing.

Deep core rules you must follow

These rules stop 99 percent of problems.

Rule 1: Always say who found what.

If you use someone’s finding, write their name and year. This shows respect and honesty.

Example: "Lopez (2018) found a drop in Z when X rises. We test if this drop holds in older groups."
Rule 2: Use short, clean words.

Long lines hide copying. Short words force you to think and rewrite.

Rule 3: Show how your study is different.

This proves you bring something new. Even if idea is old, your angle can be new.

Example: "Past work tested X in mice. We test X in real patients with stage 3 disease."

Detection guide

Follow all steps. Do not skip any.

Step A: Run a match tool

Use a trusted checker. Save the full report.

Check: - Overall percent - Top matched sources - Exact lines matched
Percent alone means nothing. Read the lines.
Step B: Read the matched source

Ask: Is this common fact? Is it a method? Is it a unique idea?

Step C: Check figures and tables

Do reverse image search. Read captions. Check if structure is same.

Step D: Mark danger matches
High danger: - Unique ideas - Same structure - Same argument flow - Same figure or data layout

Fixing step-by-step

Use this every time you edit.

  1. Make a list of all matched sentences.
  2. Write beside each: Quote, Paraphrase, or Idea.
  3. Rewrite fully using clean words.
  4. Add a clear citation.
  5. Explain how your work is different.
  6. Fix figures: redraw or ask permission.
  7. Create a change log.
Before and after:
Before: "Chronic X triggers Y in all Z groups." (copied) After: "Past studies showed X may trigger Y in many Z groups (Khan 2020). We test if this link stays the same when Z ages."

Full big example

Deep, long, clear example so no one gets lost.

Scenario:

Your match report shows 28 percent. Most lines come from one review paper.

Step 1: Build match table
Sentence | Source | Why flagged | Action

Fill this for all lines. This gives clarity.

Step 2: Rewrite all unique ideas
Matched: "Y rises when X drops due to Z." Rewrite: "Past work linked drops in X with rises in Y because of Z (Singh 2019). We test if Z acts the same in older adults."
Step 3: Explain your change

Write one line: "We changed lines 12–18 to remove copied structure and added citations."

Image and table rules

Figures are often misused. Follow these.

Rule 1: Never reuse without permission.

Even if you change colors, you must cite and ask.

Rule 2: Redraw with your own style.

Keep same idea but use your layout. Add: "Adapted from Author Year".

Figure caption template:
Figure 3. Title. Short text. Source: created by authors. Adapted from Author Year.

FAQ

Short and clear.

  • Can I reuse my text? Yes, but cite your old work and rewrite big chunks.
  • Is percent fixed? No. Use as a flag.
  • Can I use a figure? Only with permission or redraw.
